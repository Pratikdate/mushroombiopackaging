Deep Dive  ·  June 2026

From a heap of corn stalks to a finished, home-compostable packaging insert — our product is grown, not manufactured. Here is the complete, honest account of every stage in that journey: the science, the sweat, and the decisions that shaped what sits in your hands today.

01  —  The Raw Material: Choosing the Right Agricultural Waste

Every piece of Mushroom Bio Packaging begins not in our facility but on a farm. We source agricultural by-products — primarily corn stalks, hemp hurd, cotton gin waste, and rice husks — that would otherwise be burned or sent to landfill. The selection of substrate is not arbitrary; it is one of the most critical decisions in the entire process.

Each crop residue has a different ratio of cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin. Mycelium feeds on cellulose and hemicellulose, using lignin as a structural scaffold. We evaluate each batch of incoming agricultural waste against three criteria:

C:N

Carbon-to-Nitrogen Ratio

Must fall between 25:1 and 40:1 for optimal mycelium growth rate without contamination risk.

H₂O

Moisture Content

Target field capacity of 55–65%. Too wet and anaerobic bacteria outcompete mycelium. Too dry and colonisation stalls.

pH

Acidity Level

We target pH 6.0–7.5. Gypsum is added as a pH buffer and to improve substrate particle separation.

Local sourcing is non-negotiable. We partner exclusively with farms within 200 km of each growing facility to minimise transport emissions and support regional agricultural economies.

02  —  Substrate Preparation: Sterilisation & Pasteurisation

Raw biomass is teeming with competing organisms — moulds, bacteria, and wild yeasts — that would overwhelm our mycelium before it can colonise. Our substrate preparation line eliminates this competition through a two-stage thermal treatment.

82°C

Stage 1 — Pasteurisation

Bulk substrate is held at 82°C for 2 hours in our pasteurisation tunnel. This kills most competitor organisms while leaving behind heat-tolerant beneficial bacteria that actually support mycelium colonisation.

121°C

Stage 2 — Full Sterilisation

For our premium, tight-tolerance packaging grades, substrate is packed into polypropylene bags and pressure-sterilised in autoclaves at 121°C for 2.5 hours — eliminating every competing organism.

After heat treatment, substrate is cooled to below 30°C in a positively pressurised, HEPA-filtered room before inoculation. This cooling step is critical: introducing mycelium spawn to a hot substrate kills the culture instantly.

03  —  Inoculation: Introducing the Mycelium

Spawn — mycelium pre-grown on a carrier grain — is the biological engine of our product. We use a proprietary strain of Ganoderma resinaceum, selected after five years of strain trials for its fast colonisation speed, consistent density output, and exceptional binding strength.

Inside our ISO-clean inoculation room, workers in sterile gloves and masks mix the cooled substrate with spawn at a rate of 4–6% by dry weight. The mixture is then hand-packed or machine-filled into one of two vessel types depending on the production stage:

  • Bulk bags — large 20-litre polypropylene bags with filter patches. Used for the initial colonisation phase, where mycelium needs air exchange but must remain contamination-free.
  • Final-shape molds — custom aluminium or food-grade plastic tools machined to the exact geometry of the customer's product. Substrate is packed directly into these for the second colonisation phase.

Why two phases? Starting in a bulk bag allows us to produce a large volume of "pre-colonised" substrate efficiently in a controlled environment. Once the mycelium network is established and robust, we break up and transfer it into molds. At this point the culture is strong enough to outcompete stray contaminants without requiring an autoclave-clean mold environment — a key manufacturing efficiency gain.

04  —  Phase 1 Colonisation: Building the Mycelium Network (Days 1–5)

Inoculated bags are moved to our climate-controlled colonisation chambers, maintained at 24–26°C with 75–85% relative humidity. Over five days, the mycelium hyphae — thread-like structures one cell wide — spread through the substrate at a rate of roughly 1 cm per day.

What is happening inside the bag is astonishing. Each hypha secretes enzymes that break down cellulose into simple sugars, which the fungus uses as fuel. Simultaneously, the hyphae physically interweave and fuse, creating a three-dimensional mesh that is the structural basis of our final material. By day 5, the bag is a solid white block with no loose particles — the substrate has been transformed into a proto-composite.

05  —  Mold Transfer & Phase 2 Colonisation: Growing to Shape (Days 6–11)

This is where the product takes its final form. The colonised bulk block is broken into pea-sized crumbles and packed firmly into precision-machined molds. The packing density at this step directly controls the final product's compressive strength — denser packing produces stiffer, heavier material; looser packing yields lighter, more cushioning grades.

Once packed, the molds are sealed with a breathable polypropylene lid and returned to the colonisation chamber at 23°C for a further 5–6 days. The already-active mycelium network re-anastomoses — that is, the hyphal tips from adjacent crumbles recognise each other and fuse — binding the crumbles into a single, seamless mass that precisely mirrors the mold geometry.

No adhesives. No binders. No synthetic additives. The only "glue" is the mycelium itself — the same biological mechanism that holds the forest floor together.

Mold design is handled by our in-house engineering team using parametric CAD. Standard molds are CNC-machined from 6061 aluminium with a 1.5° draft angle to ensure easy demoulding without tearing the delicate surface skin. Custom molds for bespoke client shapes typically take 10–15 working days to produce and validate.

06  —  Drying & Deactivation: Locking the Structure

On day 11, grown parts are demoulded and loaded onto wire racks in our continuous-belt drying ovens. This is the most energy-intensive stage of production — and also the most transformative.

60°C

Initial Drying — 4 Hours

Gently removes surface moisture and slows biological activity without causing rapid shrinkage cracks.

80°C

Core Drying — 3 Hours

Drives moisture from the centre of the part. Mycelium cell walls begin to denature and permanently crosslink.

95°C

Deactivation — 2 Hours

Final high-heat bake completely kills all biological activity. The product is now shelf-stable, inert, and safe to handle.

Post-drying, parts lose 18–22% of their mass as water. This shrinkage is precisely modelled in our mold design — our CAD models are deliberately oversized by the exact shrinkage factor, ensuring the dried parts land within ±0.3 mm of the target geometry.

Oven energy is partially recovered through a heat-exchange system that pre-warms incoming fresh air using the exhaust stream, reducing net energy consumption per kilogram of product by 34% compared to a conventional direct-fired oven.

07  —  Quality Control: The Tests Every Part Must Pass

Before any part leaves our facility it passes through a six-point quality gate. Parts that fail any check are composted on-site — no defective product ever reaches a customer, and no material is wasted.

08  —  Surface Finishing & Packaging

Passed parts move to the finishing line. Depending on the product specification, they may receive one or more surface treatments:

  • Sanding — For consumer-facing parts where a smooth surface is required, parts are lightly sanded with 220-grit paper. This compresses the surface fibre and increases perceived rigidity by 12–15%.
  • Water-based lacquer coating — A single coat of plant-derived lacquer is applied for water-splash resistance. This does not affect compostability — the lacquer is EN-13432 certified and breaks down in home compost alongside the mycelium matrix.
  • Velvet texturing — Our luxury packaging line undergoes a proprietary surface-growth technique where, in the final 24 hours of mold colonisation, humidity is briefly raised to stimulate surface primordia — fine aerial hyphae that create a naturally velvety texture requiring no synthetic flocking.
  • Laser marking — Client branding, part numbers, or compostability symbols are laser-engraved directly into the surface — no inks, no labels, no adhesives.

Finished parts are nested and packed in recycled cardboard, wrapped in unbleached kraft paper, and labelled with water-soluble ink. The outbound packaging itself generates less than 1% of the carbon footprint of the product it protects.
