The global cold-chain logistics sector relies almost entirely on EPS foam coolers — an environmental catastrophe that produces billions of unrecyclable containers per year. Grown mycelium thermal liners match EPS performance and compost completely when the journey ends.

The Cold-Chain Problem

Temperature-sensitive shipments — pharmaceutical samples, premium seafood, meal-kit deliveries, fresh produce, and biologics — require packaging that can maintain a target temperature range for 24–72 hours without any active refrigeration. Today, the default solution is an EPS foam box. In the United States alone, approximately 150 million EPS coolers are used and discarded each year. Fewer than 10% are ever recycled. The rest enter landfill, waterways, or ocean systems, where they fragment into microplastics.

Real-World Result: Premium Meal-Kit & Pharma Cold Chain

Multiple meal-kit delivery companies in the United States and Europe have piloted mycelium thermal liners as a direct EPS replacement. Documented outcomes from industry trials include:

  • Customer satisfaction scores improved by an average of 18 percentage points when the "home-compostable" claim was clearly printed on the packaging, driven primarily by millennial and Gen-Z consumer cohorts who self-reported packaging sustainability as a key purchase loyalty factor.
  • Zero temperature excursions reported in 48-hour transit tests when mycelium liners were paired with phase-change gel packs — matching performance parity with EPS coolers of equivalent wall thickness.
  • Carbon footprint per shipment reduced by ~65% on the packaging component alone, according to Life Cycle Assessments conducted by the Ellen MacArthur Foundation's circular economy programme.
  • Pharmaceutical applications: Biologics companies have evaluated mycelium liners for ambient-to-cold short-range courier routes (same-day), where the material's cushioning properties also protect glass vials and ampoules.

Sources: Ellen MacArthur Foundation circular economy case studies; Future Market Insights cold-chain packaging report 2024; grown.bio industry trials.
