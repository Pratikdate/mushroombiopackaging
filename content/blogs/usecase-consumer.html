The Problem With Fragile Goods Packaging

Fragile consumer goods — candles, perfumes, glassware, ceramics, artisan food jars — have historically relied on two solutions: pre-formed EPS foam inserts or masses of crinkled paper. Both fall short. EPS foam is petroleum-derived, cannot be recycled at most kerbside collections, and persists in the environment for over 500 years. Paper void-fill is bulky, adds shipping weight, and offers inconsistent protection. Neither enhances the unboxing experience that direct-to-consumer brands work so hard to engineer.

How Mycelium Solves It

Our MycoComposite™ inserts are grown inside aluminium tools machined to match your product's precise geometry. The result is a cradle that fits like a second skin — holding a candle, a bottle, or a glass sculpture exactly in place without any movement during a 1.5-metre ISTA 2A drop test. The surface finish can be left naturally textured or velvet-soft for a premium tactile experience at the moment of unboxing.

Real-World Result: Seedlip Spirits

Company: Seedlip — UK-based premium non-alcoholic spirits brand.
Challenge: Ship glass bottles internationally without EPS foam, in line with their zero-compromise sustainability ethos.

Seedlip adopted mycelium-grown protective cradles for their glass bottle range. Each insert was grown to the exact silhouette of a Seedlip bottle, providing a snug, cushioned fit that eliminated bottle movement entirely during transit. The result was a dramatic improvement in the unboxing experience — consumers consistently reported the packaging as "the most beautiful eco packaging I've ever seen" across social media, generating organic brand marketing the brand had not anticipated. Damage rates during international shipping dropped, and the transition allowed Seedlip to remove EPS foam from their entire direct-to-consumer range.

Source: World Economic Forum sustainable packaging industry report; Ecovative Design partner case study.
