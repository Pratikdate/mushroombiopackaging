Flat-pack furniture companies are among the world's largest consumers of EPS foam. Corner protectors, edge guards, and void fillers for large products represent a massive and largely invisible environmental liability. Mycelium breakaway corners solve the problem at the exact same cost point as foam — and disappear harmlessly at the end of use.

The Furniture Packaging Challenge

A standard flat-pack furniture item — a bookcase, a wardrobe, a dining table — arrives in a box that can contain up to 2 kg of EPS foam in the form of corner protectors, sheet buffers, and custom inserts. For a brand shipping 1 million units per year, that represents 2,000 tonnes of foam — almost none of which can be recycled through standard consumer waste streams. IKEA, the world's largest furniture retailer, recognised this problem explicitly and committed to eliminating polystyrene from its packaging, exploring mycelium as a direct replacement.

Real-World Context: IKEA and the EPS Transition

Company: IKEA — world's largest flat-pack furniture retailer, ~800 million customers per year.
Initiative: Announced intent to replace all EPS foam packaging, investigated mycelium as a primary alternative.

IKEA's public commitment to eliminating polystyrene from its packaging supply chain brought global attention to mycelium packaging as a viable industrial-scale alternative. Their research and testing programme with Ecovative Design demonstrated that mycelium corner protectors could match the protective performance of EPS equivalents, with the following documented considerations:

  • Performance parity confirmed in drop and crush testing for standard furniture corner protection.
  • EPS foam persistence highlighted as unacceptable for a company operating in 60 countries, many of which have introduced or are introducing EPS bans.
  • Scale pathway being explored via a global licensing model, allowing local growers to produce mycelium packaging using agricultural waste from each region — matching IKEA's requirement for localised supply chains.
  • Goal set: >90% of IKEA packaging to be fibre-based or compostable by 2028, with mycelium as a key pathway for complex 3D-formed components that cannot be replaced by flat cardboard.

Sources: IKEA Sustainability Report FY2024; Ecovative Design; Ellen MacArthur Foundation.
