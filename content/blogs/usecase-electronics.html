Server components, laptops, and precision electronics demand packaging that passes rigorous drop and vibration standards. Mycelium meets every requirement — and unlike EPS foam, it doesn't end up in a landfill at the end of its short life.

Why Electronics Is the Hardest Test

Electronics packaging has the most demanding performance brief in the industry. Inserts must absorb repeated drops without cracking, resist crushing under pallet loads, maintain structural integrity across a wide temperature range (−20°C to 60°C during transit), and pass ISTA 2A, ASTM D4169, and in some cases UN 3480 certification for lithium battery shipments. Until mycelium packaging, EPS foam was considered the only commercially viable solution at scale.

Industry scale: The electronics sector generates an estimated 14 million tonnes of EPS foam packaging waste per year globally. Most of it is unrecyclable in standard waste streams and ends up in landfill or ocean systems.

How Mycelium Competes

Our high-density MycoComposite™ grade — produced with tighter packing and a longer Phase 2 colonisation window — achieves compressive strength of ≥120 kPa at 10% deflection, exceeding standard EPS 15 foam. The material's internal porosity acts as a natural energy-absorbing lattice, distributing impact forces across the entire insert volume rather than concentrating them at surface contact points.

Real-World Result: Dell Technologies

Company: Dell Technologies — global enterprise computing manufacturer.
First product: Dell PowerEdge R710 server — one of the first computing products ever shipped in mycelium packaging.

Dell began testing mycelium packaging for its server hardware division, replacing the large-format EPS blocks used to protect rack-mounted servers during shipment. The results were significant across multiple dimensions:

  • 20 million+ pounds of packaging eliminated across Dell's sustainable materials programme (bamboo, mushroom, wheat straw), saving over $18 million in packaging costs by 2012.
  • 2 in 3 Dell shipments used sustainable packaging materials by 2015 — a direct result of the mycelium and bamboo programme.
  • Industry LCA data attributed to this class of product shows 58–72% lower carbon emissions vs. EPS foam equivalents.
  • Consumer convenience: Dell publicly highlighted the "Curb" factor — customers could place used inserts directly in their compost bin rather than searching for specialist foam recyclers.

Sources: Dell Sustainability Report; The Sustainability Consortium; Packaging Digest; Ecovative Design.
